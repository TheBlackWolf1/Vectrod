The Best Free Serif Fonts

Serif fonts are experiencing a renaissance in digital design. High-resolution displays now render fine details beautifully, and designers are rediscovering the warmth and authority that serifs bring to brands.

Playfair Display remains the most impactful free serif for headlines. Its high contrast ‚Äî thick strokes meeting hairline serifs ‚Äî creates drama that no sans-serif can match. Pair it with any clean sans-serif body font and you've got an editorial aesthetic that communicates sophistication immediately.

EB Garamond is the free alternative to the legendary Garamond typeface used in books for centuries. If you need a font that communicates "this is serious, well-researched content," Garamond is your answer. It's also remarkably economical ‚Äî it sets more text per line than most serifs without sacrificing readability.

The Best Free Display Fonts

Display fonts are the showstoppers ‚Äî designed for headlines, logos, and moments where personality matters more than neutrality. Use them sparingly and with intent.

Bebas Neue is arguably the most downloaded font of the decade for a reason. Its condensed all-caps geometry communicates strength and modernity in a way that very few fonts achieve. It works for fitness brands, tech companies, posters, and anywhere you need impact without decoration. Cinzel takes the opposite approach ‚Äî drawing from Roman inscriptions, it communicates authority, history, and premium positioning. Perfect for law firms, luxury brands, and editorial projects that need gravitas.

The Best Free Script & Handwriting Fonts

Script fonts require restraint ‚Äî they're the seasoning of typography, not the main course. Used in headlines or for specific decorative elements, the best free options rival expensive paid alternatives.

Dancing Script and Great Vibes remain the most versatile free scripts. Dancing Script's rhythm feels natural and contemporary, while Great Vibes leans more formal and calligraphic. For wedding invitations, beauty brands, or any context requiring elegance, these are your starting points.

How to Convert SVG to a Font File: Complete Guide 2025

March 2025 ¬∑ 6 min read

Creating a custom font used to require expensive software like FontLab ($499) or Glyphs ($300). In 2025, you can convert any SVG file to a professional TTF or OTF font for free ‚Äî in under 60 seconds. Here's exactly how.

What You Need Before Starting

To convert SVG to font, you need SVG files of your letterforms or icons. These can come from: Canva (export as SVG), Adobe Illustrator (Save As SVG), Inkscape (free, export SVG), or any vector drawing app. Each SVG file should contain a single glyph ‚Äî one letter, number, or symbol per file.

Step 1: Prepare Your SVG Files

The most common mistake is using SVGs with multiple paths that aren't united. Before uploading, merge all paths in your SVG into a single compound path. In Illustrator: select all paths, then Object ‚Üí Compound Path ‚Üí Make. In Inkscape: select all, then Path ‚Üí Combine. This ensures your font renders correctly at all sizes.
