Font Licensing: What Every Designer Must Know

Font licensing is one of the most misunderstood aspects of professional design. Using a font without the proper license ‚Äî even unknowingly ‚Äî can result in legal action, especially for commercial projects. Here's what you need to know.

The "Free to Download" Trap

Just because a font is free to download doesn't mean it's free to use commercially. Many fonts on sites like DaFont, 1001Fonts, and Behance are "free for personal use only." Using these in client work, on commercial websites, or in products is a license violation ‚Äî even if you downloaded them legally.

Google Fonts Are Safe for Commercial Use

All fonts on Google Fonts are licensed under either the SIL Open Font License or Apache 2.0 ‚Äî both of which explicitly allow commercial use, web embedding, and modification. All 1,149+ fonts in our library are also under these licenses.

Embedding in PDFs and Documents

Many commercial font licenses restrict embedding in PDFs or documents. If you're creating PDFs for clients or products, verify that your font license includes embedding rights. SIL OFL explicitly permits this.

Web Fonts and @font-face

Using a font via @font-face on a website counts as distribution. Many desktop font licenses don't include web rights ‚Äî you need a separate webfont license. Google Fonts can always be used via @font-face under their standard terms.
